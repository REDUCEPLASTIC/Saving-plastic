Plastic bags were given out freely at supermarkets for decades. When something is free, people do not think about its environmental cost. This led to a habit of overusing bags without thinking.

Thin plastic bags and wrappers often cannot be recycled. They melt too easily and contaminate other recyclables. Because recycling is ineffective, people continue using bags without consequences.

In Singapore, people often double-bag groceries for safety or use many small bags instead of one large bag. These habits became normal and hard to break.

Supermarkets and shops sometimes wrap items too much â€” fruits in trays, vegetables in plastic, bakery items in multiple layers. This creates unnecessary plastic usage.

Many people do not realise how much plastic they use every week. They also do not know the long-term environmental damage caused by plastic pollution.

ğŸŒ± The Role of Biodegradable Bags

ğŸŒ¿

Biodegradable bags are a promising alternative when single-use cannot be avoided. They break down faster than traditional plastic, often within months or a few years. Many are made from cornstarch, cassava, or other plant-based materials, reducing our dependence on fossil fuels. In Singapore, biodegradable bags can help manage wet waste and reduce long-term pollution in drains, beaches, and waterways.

While they should not replace reusable bags, they offer a meaningful improvement over petroleum-based plastic bags and support the transition toward a greener lifestyle.
